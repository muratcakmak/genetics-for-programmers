# A Tech Entrepreneur Used AI to Design a Cancer Vaccine for His Dying Dog. Here's How, and Why It Matters for All of Us.

---

In December 2025, Paul Conyngham knelt on the floor of a Sydney veterinary clinic, cradling an eight-year-old rescue dog named Rosie while a vet scientist prepared a syringe. The clear liquid inside was a personalized mRNA cancer vaccine. It had not been designed by a pharmaceutical company or a team of oncologists. Conyngham, a tech entrepreneur who had never taken a university biology course, had designed it using ChatGPT, AlphaFold, and Grok over two months of obsessive late-night work. Rosie licked his hand.

She had been diagnosed with mast cell cancer in 2024. Prognosis: one to six months. Mast cell tumors are among the most common malignant cancers in dogs, and Rosie's case was aggressive. Chemotherapy slowed the disease but did not stop it. Surgery removed visible tumors, but new ones surfaced within weeks. By late 2025, a tumor the size of a tennis ball had grown on her hind leg. She was losing weight. Her energy was gone. The vets were out of options.

One night, unable to sleep, Conyngham did what millions of desperate people do. He turned to an AI chatbot. But Conyngham was not a typical pet owner Googling symptoms at two in the morning. He was a Sydney-based tech entrepreneur with seventeen years of experience in machine learning and data science and a background in electrical and computing engineering. He understood data pipelines, statistical models, how to interrogate complex systems. He had never pointed those skills at biology. He typed a question into ChatGPT: "What are the treatment options for mast cell cancer in dogs?" The AI did not give him a miracle. It gave him a map: immunotherapy, genomic sequencing, personalized cancer vaccines, and a pointer toward researchers at the University of New South Wales working at the intersection of RNA science and cancer treatment.

What happened next would have been unimaginable five years ago. Conyngham paid three thousand dollars to have Rosie's tumor DNA sequenced at the UNSW Ramaciotti Centre for Genomics. He used AlphaFold, the protein-structure prediction AI whose creators won the 2024 Nobel Prize in Chemistry, to model the three-dimensional shapes of Rosie's mutated cancer proteins. He used Grok to help design an mRNA vaccine sequence targeting those mutations. UNSW scientists synthesized the vaccine. A veterinary oncologist at the University of Queensland administered it. Sequencing to vaccine design took less than two months, with an additional three months for ethics approval. By mid-March 2026, the tennis-ball-sized tumor had shrunk by roughly seventy-five percent. Most of her other tumors had reduced by fifty to seventy-five percent. She had regained her energy. She was chasing rabbits in the backyard.

That is how it happened. The more interesting question is why it matters, not just for one dog in Sydney but for anyone with a body.

## The Biology, Explained in Five Minutes

To understand what Conyngham did, you need a small amount of biology. The essential concepts are elegant and, once you see them, surprisingly intuitive.

Start with DNA. Every cell in your body, and every cell in Rosie's, contains deoxyribonucleic acid, an instruction manual written in a four-letter alphabet: A, T, G, and C (adenine, thymine, guanine, and cytosine). The human version runs roughly three billion letters long. Stretched end to end, the DNA in a single cell would reach about two meters, yet it coils so tightly that it fits inside a nucleus smaller than the period at the end of this sentence. Within those three billion letters sit approximately twenty thousand genes, segments of DNA that encode instructions for building proteins. When a cell needs a particular protein, it copies the relevant gene into a temporary messenger molecule called mRNA (messenger ribonucleic acid). That mRNA travels to a cellular machine called a ribosome, which reads the instructions and assembles the protein, amino acid by amino acid. This flow from DNA to mRNA to protein is so central to life that biologists call it the Central Dogma of molecular biology.

Proteins are the machines that actually run your body. Enzymes digest your food. Antibodies fight infections. Hemoglobin carries oxygen through your blood right now. Receptors on your cell surfaces detect hormones, neurotransmitters, threats. What makes each protein unique is not just its amino acid sequence but its three-dimensional shape, a long chain that folds into helices, sheets, loops, and pockets. The shape determines the function. Predicting how a given amino acid sequence would fold was one of the grand challenges of biology for fifty years. Laboratories spent months or years determining a single protein's structure using X-ray crystallography. Before AI, science had cataloged roughly two hundred thousand experimentally determined protein structures in the Protein Data Bank, the product of decades of painstaking work.

Cancer is broken code. When certain genes mutate (letters change, get deleted, get duplicated), the proteins they encode malfunction. Growth-regulating genes get stuck in the "on" position. Tumor suppressor genes get switched off. The result is a cell that divides without restraint. But because cancer arises from mutations, cancer cells produce proteins that differ slightly from the body's normal proteins. These mutant fragments, displayed on the cell surface, are called neoantigens, molecular fingerprints unique to the cancer. Because they are foreign, the immune system can in principle recognize and destroy cells displaying them. The problem is that cancer evolves ways to hide, to suppress immune responses, to cloak itself in normalcy. The immune system often needs help seeing the target.

An mRNA vaccine provides that help. Instead of waiting for the immune system to stumble upon cancer neoantigens, you deliver temporary instructions, an mRNA molecule, that tell the body's own cells to produce specific neoantigen proteins. The cells manufacture these proteins, chop them into fragments, and display them on their surfaces. The immune system encounters the fragments, recognizes them as foreign, and mounts a targeted attack, training T cells and antibodies to seek and destroy any cell displaying those neoantigens. The mRNA degrades within days. It does not alter DNA. It delivers a message, a wanted poster distributed to every police station in the body.

If this sounds familiar, it should. The same platform produced the Pfizer-BioNTech and Moderna COVID-19 vaccines administered to billions of people worldwide. COVID vaccines encode the spike protein of a virus. Cancer mRNA vaccines encode the unique neoantigens of an individual patient's tumor. Same technology, different target. And now, that target includes the tumors inside an eight-year-old rescue dog in Sydney.

## The AI Toolkit: What Conyngham Actually Used

Conyngham did not build a laboratory. He did not buy a centrifuge or learn to pipette. He sat at his computer and used AI tools, most of them free, to do what would have previously required a multidisciplinary team of PhD scientists and months of work.

**ChatGPT as the navigator.** The most important role AI played was as a guide. Conyngham had seventeen years of experience building machine learning models but had never studied immunology, genomics, or vaccinology. ChatGPT became his biology tutor, research navigator, and institutional connector. It explained the Central Dogma, described how mRNA vaccines work, outlined the computational pipeline for neoantigen prediction, suggested specific tools, databases, and researchers. It pointed him toward the UNSW Ramaciotti Centre for Genomics. ChatGPT did not design the vaccine. But it made the project conceivable by translating an unfamiliar domain into language and concepts a software engineer could work with.

**AlphaFold as the structural analyst.** Developed by Google DeepMind, AlphaFold solved the protein folding problem, the fifty-year quest to predict a protein's three-dimensional structure from its amino acid sequence alone. At CASP14, the biennial competition that benchmarks protein structure prediction, AlphaFold 2 achieved a median GDT score of 92.4, a level of accuracy that stunned the field. Its creators, Demis Hassabis and John Jumper, shared the 2024 Nobel Prize in Chemistry. AlphaFold has since predicted structures for over two hundred million proteins, nearly every known protein in nature, and made them freely available. Conyngham used it to predict the three-dimensional structures of Rosie's mutated cancer proteins. Not all mutations make good vaccine targets. A mutation buried deep inside a protein's core may never be visible to the immune system. A mutation on the surface, altering the shape in a way that distinguishes it from the normal version, is far more useful. AlphaFold let Conyngham see which mutations changed the protein's exterior, work that would have previously required months of laboratory crystallography.

**Grok as the mRNA designer.** Once the target neoantigens were identified and validated structurally, the final step was designing the actual mRNA molecule. Grok, the AI developed by xAI, helped design the final mRNA construct: the nucleotide sequence encoding the selected neoantigens, optimized for efficient translation and molecular stability inside the body. A vaccine design, at bottom, is a text string, a sequence of nucleotide letters (A, U, G, C) arranged in a specific order. The same capabilities that allow a large language model to work with text make it useful for designing nucleotide sequences. The output of this step was not a vial of liquid. It was a text file.

**The open-source ecosystem.** Beyond the headline AI tools, Conyngham relied on a constellation of free, publicly available bioinformatics software. NetMHCpan, from the Technical University of Denmark, predicts how well protein fragments bind to MHC molecules, the cellular machinery that displays antigens to the immune system. OpenVax and Vaxrank, open-source tools from the Mount Sinai Icahn School of Medicine, rank candidate neoantigens by their likelihood of provoking an immune response. GATK and Mutect2, from the Broad Institute, identify somatic mutations by comparing tumor DNA to healthy DNA. All free. All publicly documented. The entire computational infrastructure for designing a personalized cancer vaccine exists on the open internet.

## The Pipeline: All Six Steps

What Conyngham built was a systematic pipeline, six discrete steps, each building on the last, each combining AI tools with established bioinformatics methods.

**Step 1: Sequencing.** Conyngham took Rosie to the UNSW Ramaciotti Centre for Genomics and paid three thousand dollars for whole-genome sequencing of both healthy tissue and tumor tissue. A tissue sample goes in; a massive data file comes out, containing the precise sequence of nucleotide letters that make up Rosie's genome. You need both samples, healthy and tumor, because cancer is defined by the difference between them. The mutations that drive the disease are visible only when you compare the corrupted code of the tumor against the clean code of healthy tissue.

**Step 2: Variant calling.** With two genomes in hand, Conyngham used Mutect2, a tool in the Genome Analysis Toolkit from the Broad Institute, to systematically compare the two sequences and identify somatic mutations: changes present in the tumor but not in the healthy tissue. The output is a list of mutations, hundreds or thousands of them. Most are passenger mutations, random errors that accumulated during the cancer's growth but play no functional role. The challenge is finding the handful that matter.

**Step 3: Neoantigen prediction.** For a mutation to be a good vaccine target, the mutated protein fragment must be displayed on the surface of cancer cells in a way the immune system can recognize. The fragment must bind to MHC (major histocompatibility complex) molecules, which present protein fragments to patrolling T cells. NetMHCpan, a neural network trained on hundreds of thousands of experimentally measured peptide-MHC binding affinities, predicts binding strength. Fragments predicted to bind with an affinity below five hundred nanomolar pass the filter. The yield is thin: roughly half a percent to two percent of somatic mutations produce viable neoantigen candidates. From hundreds of mutations, you might get a dozen worth pursuing. Conyngham selected the best ten to twenty based on binding affinity, expression level, and other criteria, using Vaxrank to help prioritize.

**Step 4: Protein structure prediction.** With a shortlist of candidates, Conyngham used AlphaFold. For each mutated protein, he predicted the three-dimensional structure and compared it to the unmutated version. Does the mutation change the protein's surface in a way that the immune system can distinguish from the normal protein? A mutation buried in the interior is a poor target no matter how strong its MHC binding. A mutation that reshapes the exterior is a prime candidate. AlphaFold allowed these assessments to be done computationally, in hours rather than months.

**Step 5: mRNA design.** With the final neoantigens selected, the task was designing a single mRNA molecule encoding all of them, a polyvalent construct instructing the body to produce multiple cancer-specific proteins simultaneously. The mRNA sequence had to be codon-optimized, choosing the three-letter nucleotide codes that the cellular translation machinery reads most efficiently. It required modified nucleosides (N1-methylpseudouridine), carefully designed untranslated regions for stability and translation efficiency, a five-prime cap for ribosome recognition, and a poly-A tail for mRNA stability. Grok assisted with the design and optimization. The output, the actual vaccine, was a nucleotide sequence in a text file, sent to scientists who could turn it into physical molecules.

**Step 6: Manufacturing and delivery.** Here the digital met the physical, and Conyngham could not proceed alone. Professor Pall Thordarson at the UNSW RNA Institute synthesized the mRNA using in vitro transcription and encapsulated it in lipid nanoparticles, tiny fat bubbles that protect the fragile mRNA and ferry it into cells. Same delivery technology as the Pfizer and Moderna COVID vaccines. The finished vaccine was administered by Dr. Rachel Allavena, a veterinary oncologist at the University of Queensland, under an approved animal research protocol. That approval was not trivial: three months, a hundred pages of ethics documentation. UNSW initially denied approval, requiring revisions and additional safety justifications. Professor Martin Smith at UNSW also contributed expertise. The bureaucracy was, by Conyngham's account, harder than the science. But it was the right safeguard to have in place.

## The Results

The first injection was administered in December 2025. A booster followed in February 2026.

By mid-March 2026, the results exceeded cautious hopes. The tennis-ball-sized tumor on Rosie's hind leg, the one that had resisted chemotherapy, surgery, and every other intervention, had shrunk by approximately seventy-five percent. It was softening, flattening, retreating. Most of her other tumors showed fifty to seventy-five percent reduction. She had regained weight. Her energy had returned. Conyngham described it: "She's back chasing rabbits."

The caveats matter, and Conyngham is the first to insist on them. One tumor has not responded, a reminder that cancer is not a single disease but an evolving, heterogeneous population of cells, some of which may not display the targeted neoantigens. A second vaccine variant, targeting mutations specific to the non-responsive tumor, is in preparation. Professor Thordarson has cautioned publicly that Rosie is not fully cured. The disease could adapt, find new mutations to hide behind, recur in forms the current vaccine does not address.

Conyngham himself has been measured. "I am under no illusion that this is a cure," he has said, "but I do believe this treatment has bought Rosie significantly more time and quality of life." He is right to be careful. One dog. No clinical trial. No control group, no blinding, no statistical power. In evidence-based medicine, it is an anecdotal case report, the weakest form of evidence.

But consider what actually happened: a person with no formal training in biology used publicly available AI tools and open-source bioinformatics software to design a personalized cancer vaccine that produced measurable tumor regression in a living animal, validated by veterinary oncologists at a major research university. That does not prove mRNA cancer vaccines work (clinical trials are doing that). What it demonstrates is that the tools to design such vaccines are no longer locked behind the walls of pharmaceutical companies and elite research institutions. They are on the internet. They are free. And they are increasingly usable by people who know how to work with data.

## The Bigger Picture: Why This Matters for All of Us

Rosie's story is compelling. But if it were only about one dog, it would be a heartwarming feature, not a signal of something structural. The signal is in the convergence.

Personalized mRNA cancer vaccines are in human clinical trials now, and the early results are strong. BioNTech, the same company that co-developed the Pfizer COVID-19 vaccine, is testing autogene cevumeran, a personalized mRNA vaccine for pancreatic cancer, one of the deadliest malignancies in medicine. In early trial results, patients who mounted a strong T-cell response to the vaccine had dramatically longer recurrence-free survival. Moderna is testing mRNA-4157 in combination with Keytruda (pembrolizumab) in melanoma patients, and the combination reduced the risk of recurrence or death by sixty-five percent compared to Keytruda alone. Both companies are expanding into colorectal cancer, non-small cell lung cancer, and other solid tumors. The pipeline is accelerating. The question is no longer whether personalized cancer vaccines are possible, but how fast they become standard of care.

What makes this moment distinct is not any single technology but the convergence of three revolutions, each transformative alone, collectively reshaping what medicine can do.

The first is sequencing. The Human Genome Project, completed in 2003, cost approximately 2.7 billion dollars and took thirteen years. Today, a full human genome can be sequenced for around two hundred dollars in hours. That is a cost reduction of more than ten million fold, faster than Moore's Law, faster than any other technology curve in history. Conyngham sequenced Rosie's genome for three thousand dollars, a figure that includes veterinary-grade sample preparation and a non-standard canine reference genome. For humans, the cost is lower. When sequencing costs less than a chest X-ray, the bottleneck shifts from generating data to interpreting it.

The second is structural biology, transformed by AI. AlphaFold has predicted three-dimensional structures for over two hundred million proteins, more than a thousand times the number determined experimentally over the entire history of the field. The Protein Data Bank contains roughly two hundred thousand entries, each the product of months or years of lab work. AlphaFold compressed fifty years of experimental effort into a neural network that predicts a structure in minutes. And it is free. Understanding the molecular machinery of life is no longer rate-limited by access to synchrotron beamlines or cryo-electron microscopes. It is rate-limited by asking the right questions.

The third is mRNA technology, proven at pandemic scale. Before COVID-19, mRNA therapeutics were promising but unproven. The pandemic forced the largest real-world test of any medical technology in history. BioNTech designed its COVID-19 vaccine candidate in a single weekend in January 2020. Billions of doses were manufactured, distributed, and administered worldwide, generating an unprecedented safety and efficacy dataset. That experience did not just produce a COVID vaccine. It validated the entire mRNA platform: lipid nanoparticle delivery, modified nucleoside chemistry, manufacturing processes, regulatory pathways. Cancer researchers did not have to build a new platform. They inherited a proven one and redirected it.

Each revolution would be significant alone. Together, they produce something new: the realistic possibility of personalized medicine at scale. A world where your specific cancer, not cancer in general but the unique mutational profile of your tumor, can be sequenced, analyzed, and targeted with a bespoke vaccine, designed computationally and manufactured in weeks.

And there is a fourth element: gene editing. CRISPR-Cas9, the technology that earned Jennifer Doudna and Emmanuelle Charpentier the 2020 Nobel Prize, has moved from laboratory curiosity to approved therapeutic. Casgevy, a CRISPR-based treatment for sickle cell disease, was approved by the FDA on December 8, 2023. In clinical trials, twenty-nine out of thirty patients (ninety-seven percent) were free of painful vaso-occlusive crises. Base editing and prime editing, next-generation CRISPR variants, promise even greater precision: correcting single-letter typos in the genome without cutting both strands of DNA. We can now read the genome with sequencing, understand it with AlphaFold, instruct the immune system to act on it with mRNA, and rewrite it with CRISPR. Four capabilities, developed independently, converging into one toolkit.

But power requires responsibility, and Rosie's story illuminates the limits of democratization as clearly as the possibilities. AI makes biology navigable to non-experts. Conyngham could not have designed that vaccine without ChatGPT, AlphaFold, and Grok. But navigable is not easy, and design is not deployment. He could not synthesize the mRNA himself, formulate the lipid nanoparticles, administer the vaccine, or monitor Rosie's immune response. He needed Professor Thordarson's RNA synthesis lab. He needed Dr. Allavena's oncology expertise. He needed institutional ethics review, slow as it was, to ensure basic safety standards. AI does not replace scientists or eliminate the need for wet labs, clinical oversight, or regulatory safeguards. What it does is give more people a seat at the table, allowing a data scientist to contribute to a domain that was previously inaccessible without years of specialized training. It lowers the barrier to understanding, not the barrier to recklessness.

One more dimension deserves attention: health literacy. Understanding how DNA works, what mutations are, how mRNA vaccines function, how the immune system recognizes threats. This is not trivia. It is health literacy for the twenty-first century. Genomic profiling is becoming routine. Personalized vaccines are entering trials. CRISPR therapies are FDA-approved. Within a decade, your doctor may offer a genomic health report as routinely as a blood panel. You will be asked to make decisions about genetic testing, personalized therapies, gene editing. You do not need to become a molecular biologist. But you need to understand the basics: the Central Dogma, mutations, immune recognition. Not to become a scientist. To be an informed participant in your own health care, in an era when the tools of molecular medicine are advancing faster than the public's understanding of them.

## What You Can Do Now

If Rosie's story has made you curious, if you want to understand this world rather than just read about it, here is where to start.

**Free resources for learning.** Khan Academy offers excellent, free courses on biology, immunology, and genetics, starting from zero. If you come from a technical background, learngenomics.dev translates genomics concepts into language software engineers and data scientists already understand. A community-maintained "Genetics for Programmers" roadmap on GitHub maps out a self-directed curriculum from molecular biology basics through bioinformatics. For a structured academic experience, MIT's Introduction to Biology (7.00x on edX) is free to audit and covers the foundations rigorously.

**Tools you can try today.** The AlphaFold Server is free and open to anyone. Enter an amino acid sequence and get a predicted three-dimensional protein structure. The AlphaFold Database contains over two hundred million predicted structures, searchable and downloadable. NCBI, the National Center for Biotechnology Information, is the definitive public database for genetic sequences, publications, and biological data. The UCSC Genome Browser lets you explore the human genome interactively, zooming from chromosomes down to individual nucleotides. Rosalind.info offers bioinformatics challenges, coding problems framed as biological puzzles, designed for programmers learning biology. And sandbox.bio provides a browser-based command-line environment where you can run real bioinformatics tools without installing anything.

**The closing.** As of this writing, Rosie is alive. Her tumors are shrinking. A second vaccine variant is in preparation, targeting the one tumor that did not respond to the first. She does not know she is a landmark case in the democratization of biotechnology. She does not know that her sequencing data was processed by Nobel Prize-winning AI. She does not know that her treatment was designed by tools that did not exist three years ago. She is chasing rabbits.

A tech entrepreneur with no biology degree, a handful of AI tools available to anyone with an internet connection, a network of scientists willing to collaborate across institutional boundaries, and a dog who was supposed to die. That is not a fairy tale. It is a proof of concept, a glimpse of what medicine looks like when the tools of molecular biology are measured in dollars, not millions. When AI makes the incomprehensible navigable. When the barriers between disciplines dissolve and a data scientist can sit at the same table as a virologist, an oncologist, and a structural biologist, contributing to a problem that matters.

Rosie's story is not the end of anything. It is the beginning of an era where understanding biology is not reserved for people with PhDs. Where the cost of fighting disease is plummeting along a curve that shows no sign of flattening. And where the answer to "there's nothing more we can do" might be: "Have you tried asking an AI?"
